Provincial scale-up 2024–26 · Enhancing CHW roles · Strengthening rural PHC

Tools in use.

Community Health Workers play a vital role in bridging the gap between communities and the health system, particularly in rural primary health care. Across 2024, the VAPAR community mobilization training was delivered across Mpumalanga’s three health districts to 57 CHWs and Outreach Team Leaders — who then taught what they had learned to at least 280 others, on their own initiative. Two policy briefs set out what happened, and the route from project to system.

The tools

Value in sequence

Each tool is simple on its own. Used in order, they carry a team from a recurring problem to shared analysis, clear roles, a workable plan and a record of what to pass on — then the cycle begins again.

TOOL 01

Problem tree

Breaks a recurring problem into visible effects, immediate causes and deeper household, community, facility or system drivers.

Shared causal analysis: stigma, transport, clinic processes, poverty, communication and service barriers made visible.

TOOL 02

Stakeholder & Venn mapping

Maps actors, influence, relationships and possible support around the issue.

Clearer roles for CHWs, OTLs, operational managers, traditional leaders, families and other sectors.

TOOL 03

Action pathway

Turns the analysis into sequenced steps with named responsibilities, resources and deadlines.

An action plan for tracing, adherence support, referrals, health talks or facility-level change.

TOOL 04

Reflection & peer sharing

Reviews what changed, what blocked progress and what should be shared with others.

In-service learning, mentoring, refresher sessions and diffusion records.

Applied to real service priorities — treatment adherence · stigma · tracing and loss to follow-up · health-seeking behavior · referral coordination · household registration · community trust

The evidence

Six linked findings

The evaluation synthesized training and project documentation, dissemination materials and 22 interviews with CHWs and OTLs across 22 clinics.

1

Confidence and role clarity

Participants reported greater confidence to speak, facilitate and raise concerns, and came to understand community mobilization as part of PHC work.

2

Practical use of tools

Problem trees and action pathways helped CHWs move from naming problems to analyzing causes, identifying stakeholders and planning action.

3

Teamwork and clinic–community feedback

CHWs, OTLs and facility staff reported improved coordination around tracing, follow-up, health talks and referrals — strongest where teams and supervision were stable.

4

Peer diffusion — the multiplier effect

21 of 22 interviewees shared their training through in-service sessions, mentoring and day-to-day support. One OTL reported training 49 CHWs at Embalenhle CHC.

5

Constraints shaping sustainability

Transport, uniforms, equipment, workload, protected time and literacy variation shaped who could apply and share learning: conditions to plan and budget for.

6

Early institutional uptake

Provincial actors positioned the training within revitalized Health Promotion, Regional Training Centre alignment, in-service registration discussions and roll-out planning.

Evidence status

How to read these numbers

The evidence supports claims of feasibility, acceptability, reported capability-building, peer diffusion and early institutional uptake. Diffusion counts are self-reported minimums, training fidelity was not formally assessed, and service effects and health outcomes need further prospective evaluation.

Next phase

From project to system

The opportunity is incremental institutionalization: aligning the tools and facilitation approach with existing CHW curricula, Regional Training Centre processes, in-service training, OTL coaching and Health Promotion priorities — with a minimum enabling package behind it.

Months 1–2

Confirm provincial ownership and a technical working group across PHC, Health Promotion, HRD, RTC, district managers and VAPAR partners.

Months 2–4

Complete curriculum mapping, the facilitator package and simple quality-assurance tools for peer diffusion.

Months 4–6

Select demonstration sites; run refresher and calibration sessions for master trainers, OTLs and peer facilitators.

Months 6–12

Test the MCH/CHC adaptation in selected sites, review implementation data quarterly and refine before wider roll-out.

Next use case · VAPAR-LINC

Learning for Integrated Care: adapting the cycle for maternal and child health

Maternal and child health depends on coordination between pregnant women, households, CHWs, community health centre teams, referral hospitals and district managers. Mpumalanga Department of Health supports VAPAR-LINC, focused on increasing low-risk deliveries at CHCs, strengthening referral of obstetric complications, and coordinating the CHWs who screen pregnant women in the community.

The briefs are explicit that this is translation of an established co-learning model into integrated maternity care — MCH outcomes are for the next evaluation to demonstrate.
